URIAS-ORELLANA ET AL. v. BONDI, ATTORNEY GENERAL

SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
Syllabus
URIAS-ORELLANA ET AL. v. BONDI, ATTORNEY GENERAL
CERTIORARI TO THE UNITED STATES COURT OF APPEALS FOR THE FIFTH CIRCUIT
No. 24–777. Argued November 10, 2025—Decided March 4, 2026

Under the Immigration and Nationality Act, a noncitizen may obtain asylum if he is “unable or unwilling to return to . . . [his home] country because of persecution or a well-founded fear of persecution on account of” one of several statutorily protected grounds. 8 U. S. C. §1101(a)(42). On review of a final order of removal, the courts of appeals typically defer to administrative findings of fact that are “conclusive unless any reasonable adjudicator would be compelled to conclude to the contrary.” §1252(b)(4)(B). The question in this case is whether courts should likewise apply substantial-evidence review to the Board of Immigration Appeals’ determination whether a given set of undisputed facts constitutes “persecution” under 8 U. S. C. §1101(a)(42).

Held: Courts should review the Board’s determination that a given set of facts does not rise to the level of “persecution” under a substantial-evidence standard. Pp. 3–10.

JUSTICE JACKSON delivered the opinion of the Court.

The Court has previously interpreted this provision to prescribe a deferential, “substantial-evidence standard,” Nasrallah v. Barr, 590 U. S. 573, 584, under which administrative findings of fact are “conclusive unless any reasonable adjudicator would be compelled to conclude to the contrary,” 8 U. S. C. §1252(b)(4)(B).

Substantial evidence, we have long emphasized, is “‘more than a mere scintilla,’” but “means—and means only—‘such relevant evidence as a reasonable mind might accept as adequate to support a conclusion.’” Biestek v. Berryhill, 587 U. S. 97, 103.
